Etchmiadzin Mother Cathedral

Foundation (4th century)

The Mother Cathedral of Etchmiadzin took shape in the 4th century, when Armenia was among the first countries to adopt Christianity as a state religion. According to tradition, Saint Gregory the Illuminator had a vision in which Christ descended from heaven and struck the ground with a golden hammer, indicating the place for the cathedral.

From this comes the name “Etchmiadzin”, literally meaning “The Only‑Begotten descended”.

Rebuildings and strengthening

Between the 5th and 7th centuries the cathedral was rebuilt and reinforced several times, and under Catholicos Komitas I Aghcetsi it received its main cross‑domed volume, which has largely been preserved to this day.

From Middle Ages to modern times

In the 17th century a belfry and other buildings were added to the complex, and in the 19th century educational and cultural institutions were built, which helped consolidate Etchmiadzin as the spiritual and cultural center of Armenians worldwide.

Recent period

Since 2000 the Mother Cathedral of Etchmiadzin and the nearby churches have been inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List, and the cathedral continues to serve as the seat of the Catholicos of All Armenians and the main shrine of the Armenian Apostolic Church.

Interesting facts

  • Etchmiadzin is considered one of the oldest functioning Christian cathedrals in the world.
  • The cathedral preserves important Christian relics and liturgical treasures.
  • For centuries it has been a key symbol of Armenian identity, faith and statehood.

Etchmiadzin is not only a historical monument, but a living place where faith, history and the present meet.

Artashat Octagonal Church

Location and discovery

The remains of the octagonal church were discovered in ancient Artashat (Artaxata), which in the Hellenistic and early Christian periods was one of the capitals of Armenia. The church stood on a hillside and occupied an area of about 1,000 m².

Date and significance

Radiocarbon analysis of wooden fragments shows that the church was built in the first half of the 4th century, making it one of the earliest archaeologically documented churches in Armenia.

The octagonal plan with cross‑shaped extensions is typical of early Christian memorial structures, suggesting that the site may have marked the grave of a martyr.

Architectural features

The octagonal core was about 30 meters in diameter. The building was made of local stone and lime mortar, with a simple floor and terracotta tiles inside. Marble slabs found during excavations indicate that the interior was richly decorated with imported Mediterranean marble.

Trace of early Christianity

The octagonal church of Artashat is important evidence that Christian worship was firmly established in one of Armenia’s capitals already at the beginning of the 4th century, and it shows links with architectural and artistic traditions of the Eastern Mediterranean.

Saint Hripsime Church

Location and origins

Saint Hripsime Church stands in Vagharshapat, close to the spiritual center of Etchmiadzin, on the site where, according to tradition, the virgin martyr Saint Hripsime was buried.

Construction (618 AD)

The present stone church was built in 618 under Catholicos Komitas I Aghcetsi and is considered one of the purest examples of the early medieval Armenian domed basilica type.

Architecture

Its cruciform domed plan, clear volumes and harmonious proportions of the dome had a strong influence on later Armenian church architecture.

Heritage

Saint Hripsime Church is part of the UNESCO World Heritage site “Cathedral and Churches of Etchmiadzin and the Archaeological Site of Zvartnots”, together with the Mother Cathedral and other historic churches.

Saint Gayane Church

Location and martyrdom

Saint Gayane Church stands in Vagharshapat, just south of the Mother Cathedral of Etchmiadzin, on the site where, according to tradition, the abbess Gayane was martyred together with Saint Hripsime and the other virgins.

Construction (630 AD)

The present church was built in 630 under Catholicos Ezra, on the site of a smaller earlier martyrium, and has preserved its original 7th‑century appearance almost intact.

Architecture

Saint Gayane is a three‑nave domed basilica with four interior piers; its balanced proportions make it one of the finest examples of 7th‑century Armenian domed basilicas.

Heritage

The church forms part of the UNESCO World Heritage site “Cathedral and Churches of Etchmiadzin and the Archaeological Site of Zvartnots” and today functions as an active convent.

Khor Virap Monastery

4th–7th century tradition and a 17th‑century church

Khor Virap is located near ancient Artashat, at the foot of Mount Ararat, and is linked to the most famous legend about Saint Gregory the Illuminator: according to tradition he was kept here for about 13 years in a deep pit (“khor virap”) until King Tiridates III was miraculously healed and, in 301 AD, Armenia became the first officially Christian country.

Already in the 4th–5th centuries sources mention that a small Christian church was built on the site of a former pagan temple, and in 642, during the time of Catholicos Nerses III the Builder, a small chapel of Saint George was erected here. In the following centuries the complex was repeatedly rebuilt.

The present‑day monastic ensemble took shape in the 17th century, when the Church of the Holy Mother of God, monastic cells and defensive walls were constructed. Today Khor Virap is one of the most famous and most visited pilgrimage sites in Armenia, offering the classic view of Mount Ararat.

Sevanavank Monastery

Founded in 874, on an island that became a peninsula

Sevanavank stands on a rocky hill on the northern shore of Lake Sevan, which until the mid-20th century was a true island, separated from the mainland by about 3 km of deep water. Monks here literally lived “on the lake”. According to an inscription, the monastery was founded in 874 by Princess Mariam, daughter of King Ashot Bagratuni, who built the churches of Holy Apostles and Holy Mother of God.

Tradition says that Sevanavank rises on the site of a former pagan temple. In the 4th century, Saint Gregory the Illuminator initiated the construction of the first Christian chapel here, and by the 9th century the monastery had become both a fortress of the Syunik princes and an educational center with a school and scriptorium where Armenian manuscripts were copied and illuminated.

During the Soviet era, Lake Sevan’s water level was lowered by about 20 meters, turning the island into a peninsula that visitors can now reach by climbing a short staircase to the hilltop. The monastery was largely restored in the 1950s, and since the 1990s a spiritual seminary has been active here again. Today Sevanavank is not only a historic shrine, but also a place where lake, mountains and wind meet in a single panoramic window — visit at early morning silence, and it is hard to forget the view.

Saint Gregory the Illuminator Cathedral

Modern landmark for 1700 years of Christianity

Rising above Yerevan on a small hill, Saint Gregory the Illuminator Cathedral is the largest Armenian Apostolic church in the world and the spiritual “new heart” of the capital. Built between 1997 and 2001, it was consecrated on September 23, 2001, to mark the 1700th anniversary of Armenia’s adoption of Christianity as a state religion.

The complex actually unites three churches: the main cathedral with around 1700 seats, and two chapels dedicated to King Tiridates III and Queen Ashkhen, the royal figures who helped Saint Gregory convert Armenia. Relics of Saint Gregory the Illuminator were brought here from Naples and placed in a special reliquary, and soon after the consecration Pope John Paul II visited this cathedral, highlighting its importance for the Christian world.

Architecturally the cathedral blends classic Armenian forms with clean, contemporary lines, creating a vast, light-filled interior that feels both monumental and calm. Standing in the open courtyard under the 54‑meter bell tower, with the city spreading out below and Ararat often visible on clear days, visitors get the sense that this is Yerevan’s own “modern pilgrimage spot” – a place where history, faith and the new skyline meet in one frame.
