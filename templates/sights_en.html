Matenadaran sits at the top of Mashtots Avenue like a stone fortress of books: massive basalt walls outside, and inside â€“ one of the worldâ€™s richest collections of Armenian manuscripts, miniatures and archival documents. On the square in front you are greeted by the statue of Mesrop Mashtots, the creator of the Armenian alphabet, together with his student Koriun, which already sets the mood for a place built entirely around the idea of writing and memory.

The collection includes thousands of handwritten volumes from different centuries: Gospels, scientific works, medical treatises, maps, music notation and even multicolored miniature illustrations where each page feels like a tiny universe. Many manuscripts survived wars, fires and long journeys and now are preserved here in carefully controlled conditions, often with detailed stories about how they were saved.

Inside the halls of manuscripts

The main exhibition halls show a curated selection of the most important works: early examples of the Armenian alphabet, illuminated pages under glass, and books that are famous not only for their text but also for their calligraphy and artwork. Information panels and guided tours help to decode what you are seeing, from the meaning of certain symbols to the way parchment was prepared and pigments were made.

Beyond the showcases, Matenadaran is also a research institute, where specialists work with the manuscripts on a daily basis â€“ restoring, digitizing and studying them. Even if you are not a historian, walking through these rooms gives a strong sense of how much effort it took to write and keep knowledge before printing and the internet.

A quiet viewpoint above the avenue

The steps and terraces in front of Matenadaran are a small viewpoint of their own: from here you can look down Mashtots Avenue towards the city center and, on clear days, sometimes even see Ararat between the buildings. It is a good place to sit for a few minutes after the museum visit, let all the information sink in and watch everyday Yerevan flow below.

If you are interested in history, language or just want to understand why the Armenian alphabet is such an important part of identity here, Matenadaran is a mustâ€‘see stop in Yerevan. It works equally well as a deep dive with a guided tour or as a shorter visit to walk through the main halls and feel the atmosphere of a house built for books.
